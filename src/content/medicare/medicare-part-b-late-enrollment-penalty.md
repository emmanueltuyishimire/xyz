<p>
  One of the most surprising and financially painful discoveries for new retirees is the Medicare late enrollment penalty. If you delay signing up for Medicare Part B (Medical Insurance) when you first become eligible at age 65, and you do not have qualifying creditable coverage based on active employment, the federal government will impose a permanent surcharge on your monthly premiums. This surcharge is cumulative and does not expire—paying it for the rest of your life.
</p>
<p>
  We looked into the official regulations established by the Centers for Medicare &amp; Medicaid Services (CMS) and the Social Security Administration (SSA) to explain exactly how this penalty is calculated, who is exempt, and the steps you must take to appeal if you believe a surcharge was assessed in error. Knowing these rules is critical to protecting your retirement budget from unnecessary monthly expenses.
</p>

<h2>Understanding Medicare Part B Penalties: What the Official Rules Actually Say</h2>
<p>
  Medicare Part B covers outpatient medical services, including doctor visits, preventive care, diagnostic laboratory tests, and durable medical equipment. Under Title XVIII of the Social Security Act, enrollment in Part B is voluntary, but the federal government enforces strict financial penalties for those who delay signup without qualifying coverage.
</p>
<p>
  The penalty is structured as a **surcharge equal to 10% of the standard Part B premium for each full 12-month period** that you were eligible for Part B but went without it. Because this surcharge is added directly to your monthly premium, its actual dollar cost rises every year as the standard Medicare premium increases.
</p>

<h3>The Plain English Version</h3>
<ul>
  <li>The Part B penalty is a permanent 10% premium surcharge for every full 12 months you delay signup.</li>
  <li>For 2026, each 12-month delay adds exactly $20.29 to your monthly premium bill.</li>
  <li>Active employment group health coverage (20+ workers) is the only primary shield against the penalty.</li>
  <li>COBRA, retiree health plans, and VA benefits do not count as active employment coverage for Part B.</li>
  <li>If you delay signup for 11 months, you pay a 0% penalty; if you delay 12 months, the 10% penalty applies.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Who This Applies To: The Eligibility and Exemption Rules</h2>
<p>
  Not everyone who delays enrolling in Medicare Part B faces a penalty. The federal government provides specific exemptions based on employment status and existing health insurance:
</p>

<h3>Are you covered by a current employer's group health plan?</h3>
<p>
  <strong>Yes.</strong> If you or your spouse are actively working past age 65 and are covered under an employer-sponsored group health plan, you can delay enrolling in Part B without penalty. However, the employer must have **20 or more employees** for the plan to be primary. You will qualify for a Special Enrollment Period (SEP) to sign up for Part B without penalty at any time while working, or within 8 months of leaving the job or losing the group health coverage.
</p>

<h3>Do you have COBRA or retiree health insurance?</h3>
<p>
  <strong>No.</strong> Retiree health plans, union trust funds, and COBRA continuation coverage are NOT considered active group health plans based on current employment. If you turn 65 and rely on these policies instead of enrolling in Medicare Part B, your 8-month Special Enrollment Period does not apply, and you will begin accumulating permanent late enrollment penalties for every month you delay signup.
</p>

<h3>Do you have VA health benefits?</h3>
<p>
  <strong>It depends.</strong> While VA health benefits are considered creditable drug coverage for Part D purposes, they are NOT considered creditable coverage for Medicare Part B. If you turn 65 and only have VA benefits, you must sign up for Medicare Part B to avoid late enrollment penalties if you decide to enroll later in life. Most veterans choose to enroll in Part B at 65 to ensure they have access to non-VA doctors and hospitals.
</p>

  Under federal premium rounding rules, the final surcharge amount is rounded to the nearest ten cents. Because the penalty is permanent, if you delay enrollment for two full years, you will pay a 20% surcharge every single month for life. If the standard premium rises to $210 in 2027, your surcharge will automatically rise to $42.00 per month.

<h2>What Most Sources Don't Tell You: The Partial-Year Calculation Rule</h2>
<p>
  One of the most important elements of the Part B penalty law is that the government only counts **full 12-month periods** of delay when calculating the surcharge. This rule represents a significant planning opportunity that most sources fail to mention.
</p>
<p>
  The penalty calculation counts the number of months between the end of your Initial Enrollment Period and the month you finally enroll during the General Enrollment Period, excluding any months where you had active group employment coverage.
</p>
<p>
  If this calculation results in a number that is not a multiple of 12, the remaining months are ignored. For example, if you delay enrolling for **11 months**, you have not completed a full 12-month cycle, and your penalty is **0%**. If you delay for **23 months**, you have completed one full year but not two, meaning your penalty remains capped at **10%**.
</p>
<p>
  Retirees can use this rule to coordinate their signup timing during the General Enrollment Period. If you are 23 months late in January, enrolling immediately ensures your penalty is kept at 10%. If you delay your application until March, pushing your delay to 25 months, you will cross the 24-month threshold and trigger a permanent 20% penalty.
</p>

<h2>What You Can Do: How to Appeal a Penalty Determination</h2>
<p>
  If you receive a premium bill containing a late enrollment surcharge and believe you had qualifying coverage, you have the right to request a reconsideration. Follow these steps to file an official appeal:
</p>
<ol>
  <li>
    <strong>Request Form CMS-L564 from your former employer:</strong> Contact the human resources department of the employer that sponsored your active group coverage. Have them complete Section B of Form CMS-L564. This form serves as official federal verification of your creditable coverage dates.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Gather supporting documents:</strong> If your former employer is out of business or refuses to sign the form, gather tax records showing health insurance premiums paid, W-2 forms showing employer-sponsored health benefits, or old health insurance cards.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Submit Form CMS-40B:</strong> Fill out the application for Part B enrollment (Form CMS-40B) and attach the completed Form CMS-L564 and supporting documentation.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>File a Reconsideration Request:</strong> If the SSA has already assessed a penalty, submit a written appeal letter or Form CMS-10025 to request a reconsideration of your premium surcharge rate. Mail these documents to your local Social Security office.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Work with a SHIP counselor:</strong> If you face pushback from the SSA, connect with a counselor at the State Health Insurance Assistance Program (SHIP) by visiting <a href="https://www.shiphelp.org" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">shiphelp.org</a> or calling 1-877-839-2675 for free, unbiased, expert assistance with the appeals process.
  </li>
</ol>

<h2>State Variations and Local Assistance Programs</h2>
<div class="state-variation-box" style="border: 2px solid #0A3D3A; padding: 20px; border-radius: 8px; background-color: #F6F8FA; margin-bottom: 30px;">
  <h3 style="margin-top: 0; color: #0A3D3A;">State Medicaid and Penalty Surcharges</h3>
  <p style="margin-bottom: 0;">While late enrollment penalties are established under federal Medicare rules, state-administered Medicare Savings Programs (MSPs) can override these surcharges. If your monthly income and financial assets fall below state limits, qualifying for the Qualified Medicare Beneficiary (QMB) or Specified Low-Income Medicare Beneficiary (SLMB) program requires your state Medicaid agency to pay your monthly Part B premiums. By law, the state must pay the standard premium plus any accumulated late enrollment penalties, completely absorbing the surcharge and protecting you from out-of-pocket costs.</p>
